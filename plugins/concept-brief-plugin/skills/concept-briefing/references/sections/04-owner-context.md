# Section 4 — Owner Context

**Purpose:** Build a clear, actionable profile of the property owner — who they are, what they are trying to achieve with this asset, how they make decisions, and what they need from an operator. This profile is the foundation for Key Messages (Step 5) and shapes the tone and emphasis of the entire proposal. A weak owner profile produces generic messages; a strong one makes the proposal feel like it was written specifically for this person.

---

## Proposal Approach

Work through three inputs in sequence: relationship intelligence from the user, public research, and archetype matching. Do not attempt to draft the profile until all three are complete.

---

### Input 1 — Relationship Intelligence

Before doing any research, ask the user what they already know. This captures context that no web search can find. Ask all five questions in a single block:

1. How did this opportunity come to Kerten — inbound, outbound, referral, or an existing relationship?
2. Have you met or spoken with the owner or their representative? If so, what was discussed and what did they say they want?
3. Has the owner worked with a hotel operator before, or do they have any existing operator relationships?
4. What has the owner explicitly said about their priorities for this property — financially, creatively, or operationally?
5. Are there any internal dynamics, sensitivities, or red flags you are aware of?

If the user has limited information, note what is unknown and proceed to research. Do not stall on incomplete intake — partial information is normal.

---

### Input 2 — Web Research

Search for the following. Use the owner name and company name from Property Metadata (Step 1) as the starting point.

- Owner or developer company background, portfolio, and track record
- Recent projects, completions, or announced developments
- Any public statements on investment strategy or hospitality ambitions
- News coverage, press releases, or interviews
- LinkedIn profile if dealing with an individual (name + company + city)

Note what is not findable — a limited public profile is itself informative. A private family office with no web presence signals a Family Owned archetype operating deliberately out of the public eye.

---

### Input 3 — Archetype Matching

Read `references/kh-owner-archetypes.md`. Based on the intake and research, identify the primary archetype. If the owner clearly spans two types (e.g. a family-owned group that is also a serial developer), note both and identify which is dominant.

Use the archetype to fill gaps: inferred priorities, likely fears, and watch-outs that intake and research did not directly surface. Be explicit about what is confirmed from research or conversation versus what is inferred from the archetype.

---

### Synthesis

Combine all three inputs into the owner profile below. Write in clear prose under each field — not bullet points. Keep each field tight: 2–4 sentences maximum. If a field is genuinely unknown after intake and research, write "Not established" rather than leaving it blank or speculating.

---

## Quality Bar

**Weak:** "The owner is a regional developer based in Dubai with a portfolio of residential and commercial properties. They are interested in developing a hotel and are looking for an experienced operator."

**Strong:** "Al Rashidi Group is a second-generation family-owned conglomerate (Family Owned archetype) with a 15-property residential portfolio across UAE and KSA. This project is led by the eldest son, who is repositioning the group toward lifestyle hospitality as a deliberate evolution of the family brand. The patriarch remains the final approver but is not active in the process — any commercial terms will require sign-off that is unlikely to happen in the room. Their stated priority is brand prestige over yield; they want the property to signal where the group is going, not just generate a return."

The difference: the strong version names the archetype, identifies who actually holds decision authority, distinguishes stated from inferred priorities, and flags the watch-out before it becomes a problem.

---

## Artifact Format

    ## Owner Context

    ### Owner / Developer
    [Full name and company. Note if individual, family office, or corporate entity.]

    ### Archetype
    [Primary archetype name. Secondary archetype if applicable, with a one-line explanation of the blend.]

    ### Portfolio & Track Record
    [What they own or have built. Relevant to understanding their experience level and what they're used to.]

    ### Investment Thesis
    [Why they are doing this specific project — what they are trying to achieve with this asset.]

    ### Stated Priorities
    [What the owner has explicitly said they want, from intake or prior conversations.]

    ### Inferred Priorities
    [What research and archetype matching suggest they care about, even if not stated directly. Label clearly as inferred.]

    ### Decision-Making Structure
    [Who holds authority. Who is the day-to-day contact versus the final approver. Any relevant dynamics.]

    ### Relationship Context
    [How Kerten came to know this opportunity. Existing relationship, referral, inbound, or outbound.]

    ### Watch-outs
    [Sensitivities, red flags, or risks to the relationship or deal that the proposal team should be aware of.]
